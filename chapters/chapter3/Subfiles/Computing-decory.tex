\section{Wealth inequality aversion and the racial wealth gap}
\label{subsec:compute-measures}

\subsection{Data}

\par I use the triennial Survey of Consumer Finances (SCF), $1989$--$2022$, and restrict the sample to Black and White households in each wave. For each year, I compute weighted medians for net wealth and gross assets, and then compute $I^{O}$ and $I^{A}$ under two treatments: (i) a restricted sample that keeps only strictly positive wealth values, and (ii) a shifted full sample that adds a year-specific constant so all observations are strictly positive.

\par The \textit{ambiguity} aggregator used for $I^{A}$ follows the Gilboa--Schmeidler construction with $C=\Delta(S)$ and $S=\{\text{Black},\text{White}\}$. Because the criterion is linear in probabilities over the two states, the inner minimum is attained by putting all weight on whichever racial group has the lower within-group expected utility that year, so computationally $W^{A}_{t}=\min\{\mathbb{E}_{B,t}[U(y)],\,\mathbb{E}_{W,t}[U(y)]\}$. The wedge $I^{A}_{t}>I^{O}_{t}$ arises relative to the objective benchmark $W^{O}$ defined in Appendix~\ref{app:objective-benchmark}.

\par The main text reports $\epsilon\in\{0.25,0.5,1,1.5,2\}$; the linear benchmark $\epsilon=0$ is discussed in Appendix~\ref{app:eps0-benchmark}.

\par With these modeling objects fixed, the theoretical welfare expressions map directly into computable objects in the data: for each year and wealth concept, we compute $W^{O}$ and $W^{A}$, invert $U$ to recover the equally distributed equivalent wealth level, and then construct $I^{O}$ and $I^{A}$. The remaining step is to set values for $\epsilon$, which governs the curvature of $U(y)$ and therefore the degree of inequality aversion imposed in the computation.

\par The parameter $\epsilon$ indexes the curvature of the social welfare function $U(y)$ and therefore the strength of inequality aversion: larger $\epsilon$ means stronger concavity, a higher penalty on low-wealth outcomes, and thus higher implied inequality aversion in both $I^{O}$ and $I^{A}$.

\par For orientation, read the CRRA index in four regions: (i) $\epsilon=0$ is the affine (linear) benchmark with no inequality aversion in the objective aggregator, reported separately in Appendix~\ref{app:eps0-benchmark}; (ii) $0<\epsilon<1$ yields concavity that is still milder than the logarithmic case; (iii) $\epsilon=1$ is the log boundary; (iv) $\epsilon>1$ imposes tail sensitivity strictly stronger than the log case. The subsections below walk through $\epsilon\in\{0.25,0.5,1,1.5,2\}$ so that each reported value sits in a clearly labeled part of this map.

\par Checking gross assets alongside net wealth is useful because it verifies that the empirical trend is not an artifact of liability measurement alone: both series move with similar broad dynamics over time. At the same time, the gross-asset gap is higher in levels than the net-wealth gap, which is consistent with definition since net wealth subtracts debts while gross assets do not.

\par Here $\epsilon=0.25$ lies strictly between the linear benchmark and $\epsilon=1$: concavity is present but still relatively mild compared with the log case.

\par Even with this low curvature, the ambiguity ranking already yields higher inequality levels than the objective ranking in both wealth concepts (\(I^{A}>I^{O}\)); the restricted sample is higher and smoother over time, while the shifted full sample is more cyclical.

\par Relative to the higher-curvature specifications ($\epsilon=0.5,1,1.5,2$), the $\epsilon=0.25$ series are lower in level, which is exactly what we should expect when inequality aversion is weaker.

\par Still in $0<\epsilon<1$, this specification moves further toward the log boundary than $\epsilon=0.25$, so low-wealth outcomes receive a larger welfare penalty.

\par For $\epsilon=0.5$, the restricted sample produces high and comparatively stable inequality levels over time, while the shifted full sample varies more across years. In both net wealth and gross assets, $I^{A}$ is consistently above $I^{O}$, so the objective benchmark understates measured inequality relative to the ambiguity benchmark.

\par At $\epsilon=1$, the homothetic specification coincides with logarithmic utility, the usual boundary between ``below log'' and ``above log'' curvature in this family.

\par The same ranking persists at $\epsilon=1$: in every case, $I^{A}>I^{O}$. Relative to $\epsilon=0.5$, the restricted-sample indices move upward in both wealth concepts, while remaining smoother over time than the shifted full-sample series.

\par With $\epsilon>1$, curvature is strictly stronger than at the log benchmark, so the social welfare functional penalizes low wealth sharply.

\par In the shifted full sample, this is where the pull-up becomes clear: levels rise materially relative to $\epsilon\in\{0.25,0.5,1\}$, although the series is still not uniformly near one in all years. In the restricted sample, both indices remain very high and smooth, with $I^{A}>I^{O}$ preserved.

\subsection{$\epsilon=2$: strongest curvature in the main text}

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.92\linewidth]{\subfix{../Figures/07_measures_eps2_net.png}}\\[0.75em]
\includegraphics[width=0.92\linewidth]{\subfix{../Figures/07_measures_eps2_gross.png}}
\caption{Inequality measures for $\epsilon=2$ (top: net wealth, bottom: gross assets). In each panel pair, shifted full sample is on the left and restricted sample is on the right.}
\label{fig:eps2-measures}
\end{figure}

\par This is the largest value of $\epsilon$ reported in the main text, so it pushes the homothetic specification closest to an extreme concern for the lower tail of wealth.

\par As $\epsilon$ rises from $1.5$ to $2$, restricted-sample inequality indices concentrate even more tightly near one, reflecting the stronger curvature penalty on lower-wealth outcomes. Even in this high-curvature case, the ambiguity measure remains weakly above the objective measure, preserving the core result $I^{A}\ge I^{O}$ throughout.

\par Taken together, the inequality-measure series tell the same within-sample story across specifications (from $\epsilon=0.25$ through $\epsilon=2$): inequality remains persistently high over time, with fluctuations but no sustained convergence. This is consistent with Figure~\ref{fig:gap-net-gross}, which shows a persistently positive Black-White median wealth differential throughout the sample.

\par In the restricted positive-only sample, measured inequality is especially robust to specification in the sense that levels remain high across all reported $\epsilon$, and the indices move close to one as curvature rises (most clearly for $\epsilon\ge 1$, and for both $I^{O}$ and $I^{A}$ at $\epsilon=2$). Episodes on the shifted series where \textit{both} $I^{O}$ and $I^{A}$ dip together are driven mainly by the shift treatment (year-specific $c_{t,m}$ and the changing mass at non-positive wealth), not by the maxmin step alone; the latter chiefly adds the systematic wedge $I^{A}>I^{O}$. Stronger curvature also pushes both indices upward. By contrast, in the shifted full-sample treatment both $I^{O}$ and $I^{A}$ are more variable over time and show episodes of comparatively low measured inequality at low and moderate $\epsilon$; they begin to pull upward noticeably at $\epsilon=1.5$, and become frequently near one only in the highest-curvature case $\epsilon=2$. This higher time variation in the shifted series tracks the median-gap dynamics in Figure~\ref{fig:gap-net-gross} more closely than the restricted-sample series. Appendix Figure~\ref{fig:shift-mass-diagnostic} plots the shift constant and the weighted share with non-positive wealth, and Appendix Figure~\ref{fig:gap-net-gross-restricted} shows that, even under the same positive-only restriction, both net-wealth and gross-assets median gaps still vary over time and trend upward; relative to that diagnostic, the restricted-sample theoretical indices are unusually stable and persistently high, with $I^{A}$ near the top throughout and $I^{O}$ increasing as $\epsilon$ increases.
